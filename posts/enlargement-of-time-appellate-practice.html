Enlargement of Time in Appellate Practice: A Delicate Subject (Part I)

Have you heard of a Motion for Enlargement of Time? The answer is certainly yes for my legal colleagues and probably no for non-lawyers who are not familiar with much legal jargon. When the law requires an action to be performed, it attaches a time within which to perform that action. But humans being humans, we do not always accomplish a targeted task within the time allotted by the law. It is here that the motion for enlargement of time comes in.

What the Law Provides

The motion for enlargement of time is provided for in Section 1.7(2)(a)(b) of the Civil Procedure Law. Under subsection (a), you may request the court to give you more time to complete an action, provided you have good cause to show.

What if you did not request the court to extend the time and you could not perform the targeted task within the original time allotted by the law — what do you do? Here, you take advantage of subsection (b), which operates on the primary premise that although you were neither able to request the court for more time nor able to perform what you ought to have performed within the original time, the law will still give you time to do so because your failure was excusable. The law characterizes this kind of failure as excusable neglect.

I consider enlargement of time a genuine innovation in the Civil Procedure Law.

The Appellate Complication

Notwithstanding, my observation of cases handled by the Supreme Court of Liberia shows that enlargement of time, when applied to Section 51.4 of the Civil Procedure Law — which governs taking an appeal from a lower court to the Supreme Court — becomes a genuinely delicate subject for several reasons.

The Bill of Exceptions Deadline

The first issue concerns the filing of the bill of exceptions. The bill of exceptions — the compilation of your argument to the judge of the trial court who ruled against you, notifying him why you believe his ruling was erroneous — must be filed within 10 days of receiving the ruling. What if you could not file the bill of exceptions within 10 days? Do you file it anyway, without requesting enlargement of time, on the doctrine of excusable neglect? I think the prudent course is to request enlargement of time, and if granted, proceed to file your bill of exceptions.

In a line of cases, this question has been answered differently. Lawyers proceeded to file their bills of exceptions outside of the 10-day period without first seeking enlargement of time. Surprisingly, in similarly situated instances, the lawyers on the winning side did not ask the trial court to dismiss the appeal — they waited to do so before the Supreme Court.

SRIMEX v. LPRC: A Recent Precedent

This was precisely the situation in SRIMEX v. LPRC, Supreme Court Opinion, March Term 2026, recently delivered by Justice Kanneh. SRIMEX had prevailed in a petition to compel arbitration. LPRC appealed but filed its bill of exceptions two days late. SRIMEX filed before the Court a Motion to Dismiss for the late filing. LPRC conceded to the late filing but argued excusable neglect and requested enlargement of time. The Court held that the Motion to Dismiss ought to have been filed before the trial court, and that the neglect arising from the illness of appellant's counsel was excusable.

A Dissent Worth Noting

Though this is a recent precedent, I must note that Justice Wolokolie disagreed and dissented on the ground that enlargement of time should have been requested in the trial court. In her dissent, she reiterated her reasoning in Harris v. Barkemeni — the opinion relied on by the Majority — where a noncompliant appellant requested enlargement of time to perfect the appeal. In Harris, the appellant's noncompliance was primarily because the trial judge, who should have approved the bond, had traveled from his Circuit in Maryland County to Monrovia. The Court refused to allow the appellant to suffer injury for what was attributable to the court.
