Land Reform as Restoration: A Liberian Argument from the University of the Western Cape

How can a nation approach land reform if it must also prevent the recurrence of war? That question carried me to South Africa. It is a transitional justice question, and it is one I was determined to pursue with the rigour it deserves.

In 2009, Liberia's Truth and Reconciliation Commission presented its findings. Among its conclusions was a finding that tension over land had been among the causes of the Liberian Civil War. That finding settled something in my mind that I had long suspected from my years in land advocacy: land in Liberia was not merely an economic or administrative question. It was a justice question. And justice questions require justice frameworks.

The Western Cape and the TRANSCRIM Programme

I entered the University of the Western Cape (UWC) with a clear focus: post-conflict land reform programmes and the legal frameworks that could support them without sowing the seeds of future conflict. The Faculty of Law's LLM in Transnational Criminal Justice (TRANSCRIM) positioned me to pursue exactly that.

The TRANSCRIM programme combined two intensive modules: International Criminal Law and Transitional Justice. Through these, I studied procedural and substantive global criminal justice with a focus on the post-World War II criminal tribunals, including Nuremberg, Tokyo, the International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia, the International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda, the Special Court for Sierra Leone, and the International Criminal Court. Each tribunal presented its own model of accountability, its own theory of what justice after atrocity should look like.

What became clearer to me through that study was a distinction that would reshape my thinking about land reform entirely: the distinction between retribution and restoration. Retributive justice focuses on punishment for past wrongs. Restorative justice focuses on repairing the harm those wrongs caused. For a nation rebuilding after civil war, and for communities whose relationship to land had been broken by law and policy over generations, restoration was not just morally preferable. It was strategically necessary.

The Argument: Liberia's Land Reform as Restorative Justice

Armed with this lens, I developed an argument that I believe holds up to scrutiny. Land reform in Liberia, by giving statutory recognition to customary land ownership and establishing a central land regulatory agency to enforce these rights, adopted a restorative path. It can properly be characterised as a form of land restitution.

To understand why, one must understand the legal framework that preceded the Land Rights Act of 2018. Under the Public Land Sale Law of 1956, public land was defined as any land not held under a privately registered deed. The consequence of that definition was stark: customary communities, who had lived on and governed their lands for generations, held no legally recognised ownership. They could be, and were, displaced in the interest of concessions. The law did not protect them. In many instances, the law was the instrument of their dispossession.

This changed in 2018. The Land Rights Act statutorily recognised community land ownership for the first time in Liberia's modern legal history. For me, this was not merely a reform. It was a restoration.

What Was Restored

Prior to the arrival of settlers and the formation of modern Liberia, indigenous communities owned the land. That ownership was progressively extinguished by state policy, including the 1847 Constitution and the 1956 Public Land Sale Law. The legal recognition that followed in 2018 did not create a new right. It restored one that had been systematically removed. Seen through the lens of transitional justice, the Land Rights Act addressed a historical dispossession using the instruments of law, exactly as restorative justice frameworks prescribe.
