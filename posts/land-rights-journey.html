A Memory That Still Lives: My Journey in Land Rights Advocacy

Land rights is not merely a subject I have worked on. It is, in a very real sense, a part of who I am. Over a decade has passed since I first joined the queue of advocates working on land and natural resource governance in Liberia, and yet the journey still sounds like yesterday. The question of land has become like blood running through my veins: who owns it, who is protected by law, and who is most often left out.

Where It Began

In 2010, I graduated from the University of Liberia with my first degree in Sociology. The question that followed, "what next?", would set the course of the next decade of my life. I entered law school, and in that same period, I chose to volunteer with ACORD, the Association of Community Rights Defenders.

That decision to volunteer, which might have seemed modest at the time, turned out to be one of the most consequential choices I have ever made. With ACORD, I went deep into the work of Community Forest Rights, primarily championing the implementation of the 2009 Community Rights Law, itself an outgrowth of the National Forestry Reform Law, with the goal of establishing community forests and placing the governance of Liberia's forestry resources in the hands of the communities that depend on them.

We collaborated with USAID PROSPER and the Forestry Development Authority, and as part of that work, I attended the Community Forestry Working Group, a multi-stakeholder body that brought together civil society, government, and the donor community around the shared challenge of forest governance. That chapter ran through 2013 and shaped my understanding of how advocacy, policy, and implementation must work together if change is to be real and lasting.

From the Forest to the Policy Table

In 2013, I moved from community-level advocacy into land policy, joining the Liberia Land Commission as Deputy Program Officer for Land Law Reform. In that role, I worked to support the consultation and drafting of two of the most significant pieces of land legislation Liberia has produced: the Liberia Land Authority Act and the Land Rights Act.

The formal mandate of the Land Commission ended in 2015, but my attachment to the land sector was only deepening. I joined the Land Governance Support Activity (LGSA) as a Project Consultant, working to document how the key land instruments (the Land Rights Policy, the Land Rights Act, and the Land Authority Act) had been developed and what their implementation would require.

Managing the Flagship

In November 2016, I was recruited by USAID Liberia to serve as the primary project supervisor for the very LGSA project I had earlier consulted for. It was a $15.6 million initiative with a clear mandate: support the legislation of the Land Rights Act and then ensure that the newly established Land Authority had what it needed to implement the law.

We succeeded. I managed the implementation of LGSA from 2016 through to its closure in 2021, a period that saw the Land Rights Act signed into law and the institutional architecture around it begin to take shape. Upon closure, I embarked on the design of its successor, the Land Management Activity (LMA), a $10 million project.

Though LMA ended prematurely with two years remaining in its implementation cycle, it achieved significant results. It successfully implemented the Community Land Rights Provision of the Land Rights Act and delivered approximately 12 Customary Land Deeds to communities within its implementation area. The gains built through these projects remain a strong pillar of Liberia's land sector to this day.

A Memory That Still Lives

When I look back across this journey, from the sociology graduate asking "what next?" to the policy architect of landmark land legislation, I am struck by how much a single decision to volunteer can set in motion. The land sector gave me purpose, shaped my legal thinking, and connected me to communities whose lives are directly determined by whether the law protects them or ignores them.

Among the dimensions of this work that I hold most closely is the fight for women's land rights, a cause that sits at the intersection of law, gender equity, and social justice, and one that remains unfinished.
