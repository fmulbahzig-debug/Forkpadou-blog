Theory Meets Practice: A Criminology Class at the University of Liberia Visits the Monrovia Central Prison

Reflections from a Penology (CRIM 301) Class, University of Liberia

It was a Saturday morning in March 2026 on the Fendall Campus of the University of Liberia. I entered ACG 1-30, popularly known as the Chinese Building, introduced myself, and wrote on the board, in bold: P-E-N-O-L-O-G-Y (CRIM 301).

My mission was straightforward: to introduce and hold a foundational discussion on Penology, a junior course in criminology that I would be teaching for the duration of the semester. Penology focuses on the principles, purposes, and methods of punishment as a response to criminal behaviour. It examines those aspects of the criminal justice system concerned with punishment, prisons, and penal reform.

Our discussions have been well grounded in theory, and the students have engaged the course with noticeable enthusiasm. My goal at the University has always been to simplify complex lessons and make learning accessible, and with that goal in mind, it became a duty to marry theory with practice, ensuring that my students visited the Monrovia Central Prison (MCP), where they would see the practical side of the lessons they had encountered in the classroom for over three months.

The Visit

On June 6, 2026, I, along with my class, 27 in number, visited the Monrovia Central Prison. At the bare minimum, the visit enabled students to locate the MCP for the first time; for those who knew the location, to enter the compound for the first time; and for those who had entered before, to tour the various facilities under the guidance of a knowledgeable team of professional prison administrators. The students coordinated well among themselves, making voluntary contributions to secure sanitary items for the inmates. Student Aaron Jelleh, the Visit Coordinator, made a compassionate presentation of the items to the Prison Superintendent. At the front of a horizontal queue of his colleagues, he pointed to the items, comprising one large bottle of Clorox, one bag of tide soap, two bottles of air freshener, one large pack of sanitary pads, and one bag of tissue, and said:

"We are very happy that you allowed us to come today to learn what we have been discussing in class with our instructor. As a token of our appreciation, we present these items in good faith that you can share them with the inmates."

Student Aaron Jelleh presenting sanitary items to the Prison Superintendent on behalf of the CRIM 301 class

Inside the Compound

On entering the prison compound, one message became clear: freedom should never be taken for granted. It is Saturday, 11:30 a.m. The weather is pleasant, as evidenced by the emerging sun. Saturdays are noted for worship. In fact, we are greeted by the thunderous sound of a worship drum, harmonious with the complementary sounds of the sasa and the hand claps. Though not directly in the worship room itself, I sense a profoundly devotional atmosphere. We had first been led to the women's wing, so the worshippers are a group of women who, in my view, are never short of hope that freedom is in sight.

My students and I were fortunate to meet one of the caretakers for the wing, who was quite welcoming. "These are students from the University of Liberia who are here on a learning visit," the Superintendent informed her. She was right on top of the statistics displayed in white inscriptions on a wide chalkboard. She walked us through what was recorded there. As of our visit, there were 81 female inmates: one sick person who had been taken to the JFK Hospital, five juveniles, and 75 adults. The statistics also vividly narrated one of the most significant challenges in the country's prison system: out of the 81 women, only 10 had been convicted. The remaining 71 were pretrial detainees.

The Pretrial Detention Problem

There is no certainty as to when the pretrial detainees will be freed or convicted.

That question does not rest with the prison.

The question borders on a larger systemic challenge that partly rests with the court but ultimately requiring a comprehensive interagency response, with a whole-of-state approach.

Many of these pretrial detainees languish in prison the longest with no opportunity to face their accusers in court. Many committed offences that are bailable, yet they cannot afford the cost of bail.

In the classical criminological tradition, Cesare Beccaria, whose foundational work On Crimes and Punishments (1764) shaped modern criminal justice thinking, argued that punishment must be certain, swift, and proportional in order to serve as an effective deterrent. Critically, Beccaria held that it is the certainty of punishment, not its severity, that deters crime. When pretrial detainees spend years in custody without trial or conviction, neither certainty nor swiftness is achieved. Inevitably, what steps in becomes the state of the country's human rights status. To that end, a damning human rights image is projected on the pages of international reports that brand the country as the perpetrator of human rights violations, as opposed to a nation struggling to punish crimes.

The Broader Picture

The numbers from the women's wing are a microcosm of the total prison population. Out of 1,599 inmates, 1,211 are pretrial detainees. Only 388 have been convicted. This is alarming, but perhaps what is even more alarming is that the prison was originally built to accommodate 374 persons. It has long exceeded its intended capacity. That is a challenge demanding immediate attention from policymakers.

What Works: Classification and Record-Keeping

As obvious as the challenges are, it is not all gloomy. The records of inmates are handled with diligence. A dual record system is in place: identifying information is kept both manually and in digital form. On entry, inmates are classified by the nature of their offence and categorized as high, medium, or low risk, a system that determines which holding facility a person is assigned to. Beyond this risk categorization, the system accounts for adults and juveniles, females and males, and sick and healthy inmates.

"What happens when an inmate dies in the holding facilities?" one of my students wanted to know. The answer was succinct: prison administrators do not have the medical authority to pronounce death. Suspected cases are referred for medical examination, and when death is confirmed, administrators consult their records; each inmate is registered with the names of emergency contacts, who are then notified to take delivery of the body.

This classification system and the capacity for robust record-keeping represent a significant dimension of prison reform that Liberia should acknowledge with pride. It is consistent with established theory. The English prison reformer John Howard, in his seminal work The State of the Prisons in England and Wales (1777), made extensive recommendations for prisoner classification, specifically advocating for the separation of prisoners by sex, age, and category of offence. What is now in practice at the MCP reflects, in meaningful ways, the principles Howard championed over two centuries ago.

Life Beyond the Cell

The compound was lively, with inmates taking their sunbath and gathered in smaller groups. "Sunbath is a right and entitlement for each inmate, as good behaviour may determine," the Chief Investigator noted. Inmates are not simply confined to dark rooms all day; they have engagements among themselves within the compound.

We were also ushered to the section housing the male juveniles, 14 in number. It is an enclosed area toward the back of the compound, somewhat isolated from the adult sections. The juvenile holding facility is connected to a wired basketball court, where they exercise among themselves. These juveniles had recently seen a change in leadership: their "Five Star" had turned 19 and been reclassified as an adult. Another juvenile, first name intentionally withheld, with the last name Boakai, recently stepped in as the new Five Star. He spoke on behalf of the group. As he stood, one of his peers shouted, "President Boakai!" Young Boakai, 16, looked lively. "I am the President," he told us, "but even the President can be disciplined when he goes wrong."

"I am the President, but even the President can be disciplined when he goes wrong."

Juvenile detainee Boakai, age 16, Monrovia Central Prison

Health and the Kitchen

Health remains a concern on the compound. Sick inmates are first treated at the clinic on-site. Where their conditions exceed the clinic's capacity, they are transferred to JFK Hospital. Those with communicable diseases such as tuberculosis are housed in a separate facility on the compound and given special attention until their recovery.

Adjacent to the clinic is the kitchen, known on the compound as the "Powerhouse", where all meals are prepared. Some students had held the notion that "South Beach," the popular name for the MCP, is actually "Salt Beach", a misconception rooted in the belief that food at the prison is deliberately salty as a form of punishment. Patricia, the dietician, who has served the MCP for 20 years, firmly dismissed this. "Here is the food; it's not salty, you can taste it if you want," she told us. "All of us on this compound eat the same food prepared for the inmates," the Superintendent added. On the day of our visit, the kitchen was preparing 29 bags of 25-kilogram rice for the 1,599 inmates. Each inmate is entitled to a board, a triangular rubber tray with sealed compartments. Selected inmates assist with food preparation, chosen on the basis of good behaviour.

CRIM 301 students at the gate of the Monrovia Central Prison

Reflections

After the visit, the practical side of theory had been experienced, and perspectives had shifted. "The views people hold about South Beach are different from what is actually on the ground," Daniel Whayne, my special assistant, observed. However, it must be noted that the only thing heard beforehand that was verified on the ground is the fact that the prison is overcrowded and holds a great many pretrial detainees.

The condition of overcrowding in Liberia's prisons should not be ignored. The findings of this visit are a call to action: what proportion of the national envelope should be set aside for human-centred institutions such as prisons is a question that policymakers must take seriously, and the time to answer it is now.
