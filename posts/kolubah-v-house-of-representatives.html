Kolubah v. House of Representatives: A Summary of the Ruling by Justice Kaba

Access to timely public information is my goal. Consistent with that goal, I have provided access to the case Kolubah v. House of Representatives, as recently delivered by Justice Kaba.

Background

Hon. Kolubah filed a Writ of Prohibition requesting the Court to prohibit and restrain the House of Representatives from expelling him. Despite the filing, the expulsion proceeded — and it is that expulsion which gave rise to this bill of information.

The Bill of Information

Hon. Kolubah, as informant, filed a bill of information before the Justice in Chambers, requesting that the Court reverse his expulsion and hold the House members in contempt for disrespecting the Court's order.

The Arguments

The House of Representatives argued that the Writ of Prohibition was never served on it, and that had the Writ been properly served, it would not have proceeded with the expulsion. In support of its position, the House cited Rule 26.5 of the House Rules, which requires the Sergeant-at-Arms to sign for all documents received and turn them over to the Chief Clerk.

The Marshal's returns, however, showed that Speaker Koon was served through his receptionist, who signed and acknowledged receipt in the presence of the Chief Clerk. The Chief Clerk then instructed her computer operator to take the necessary action, and the operator signed and received a copy of the Writ.

The Ruling

The Chambers Justice denied the information, ruling that service was not proper. The Justice further held that the informant, having knowledge of the House Rules, was under an obligation to identify the proper person authorized to receive court precepts — an obligation he failed to fulfil.

Next Step

The informant has appealed to the full Bench. The pendulum has now shifted to the Bench, which will examine the Ruling of the Justice in Chambers and make a final determination.
