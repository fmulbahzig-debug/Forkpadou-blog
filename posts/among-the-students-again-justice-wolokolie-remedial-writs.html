A Special Presentation at the Liberia School of Law: Justice Wolokolie on Liberia's Remedial Writs

Law Clerk, Chambers of Associate Justice Jamesetta Howard Wolokolie, Supreme Court of Liberia · Monrovia

Today, June 8, 2026, my principal, Associate Justice Jamesetta Howard Wolokolie, visited the Liberia School of Law on Capitol Bye Pass to deliver a presentation to the Civil Procedure class on invitation from former Chief Justice Francis Korkpor. The subject was one that every Liberian lawyer carries from the classroom into practice and never quite leaves behind: Remedial Writs.

Accompanying her was a gracious privilege. It was also a quiet homecoming to lessons I first met as an undergraduate. Since taking up my duties as a clerk in her Chambers, I had heard of the years when she lectured at the University of Liberia, of how meticulous she was, how she asserted control over the language.

Moments like these do not easily repeat themselves, and so I took a seat among the students, listened to the way I once did as a student, and resolved to write down notes from her presentation.

What followed was my courage to document her presentation: partly for the students who were there, partly for those who were not, and partly for myself.

Setting the Foundation

She did not begin with cases. She began, as good teachers do, with the question underneath the question: what is a remedial writ, and why does it exist?

Remedial writs, she explained, are the extraordinary judicial processes by which the Supreme Court supervises the courts, tribunals, public officers, and administrative agencies beneath it. They are not ordinary remedies. They are reserved for the moments when the ordinary remedies, chiefly the right of appeal, are inadequate or simply unavailable. Their purpose is to correct, remove, or lessen a wrong, fault, or defect, or to enforce an existing substantive right. For their home in our law she pointed the class to Chapter 16 of the Civil Procedure Law, Special Proceedings, Sub-Section B, and to Part 7 of the Revised Rules of the Supreme Court.

Then she named the five remedial writs: Certiorari, Mandamus, Prohibition, Error, and Quo Warranto. Watching her move through them in sequence, I understood that the structure itself was a teaching device: each writ defined, then its requirements, then the line between where it will lie and where it will not.

Certiorari

Certiorari, she said, is a special proceeding to review and correct the decisions of officials, boards, or agencies acting in a judicial capacity, or to review an intermediate order or interlocutory judgment of a court. The emphasis fell on that word, interlocutory. Certiorari is a writ for matters still in motion, for the ruling made while the case is alive, not the judgment that ends it.

She walked through what a petitioner must show: that he is a party to the proceeding below; a statement of the decision or interlocutory ruling alleged to be illegal; the certification of two members of the bar that the contention is sound in law; service on the opposing party as respondent; payment of accrued costs and, where required, a bond. She noted that the issuance of the writ acts as a stay of the proceedings below.

Where the writ will lie, she returned to the Court's own language: that certiorari "erases what is erroneous and substitutes in its place that which is correct" to ensure substantial justice. She used Rogers v. Thorpe and the recent Carmmo case to show the writ correcting an interlocutory misstep while a matter was still pending.

Where it will not lie was, she expanded so well in ways that captured my undivided attention. Certiorari is not a substitute for an appeal. It will not ordinarily be granted to review an interlocutory ruling on a pure question of law, nor where the ruling is not manifestly prejudicial and relief can still be had on regular appeal. Here she drew on CFAO v. Cooper, Abi-Jaoudi v. Pearson, Morris v. Flomo, and the 2025 Delia Wholesale matter, the last a clean illustration of the writ being refused because the ruling complained of was final, not interlocutory, and the petitioner had other remedies she had failed to pursue.

Mandamus

If certiorari corrects, mandamus compels. She defined it simply: a special proceeding to obtain a writ requiring the respondent to perform an official duty.

The three conditions, she indicated, sound poetic and worth memorizing: the petitioner must have a clear legal right to have the act done; it must be the legal duty of the respondent to perform it; and there must be no other plain, speedy, and adequate remedy. One example landed with particular weight, because it was an opinion she delivered for the Supreme Court: Unity Party & Liberia People's Party v. National Elections Commission, the 2023 case in which the Court held that the National Election Commission had a mandatory, non-discretionary duty to make the Final Registration Roll available before the elections. She also reminded us that the granting or refusing of an appeal is not left to a trial judge's discretion; where a judge refuses, mandamus will lie to compel him, as in Caine v. Yancy. And from Clarke v. Minister of Finance, she drew the principle that mandamus is the proper writ to obtain just compensation when government takes a citizen's property.

But mandamus, too, has its limits. It will not be used to review an exercise of judicial discretion where an adequate remedy exists (Harmon v. Horace); it will not enforce private contractual rights dressed up as public duties (Republic v. Leadership of the Liberian National Bar Association; Park v. Hanil Marines); and it will not issue to revise a court's discretionary refusal of an injunction in an election controversy (Coleman v. Beysolow). The lesson, as she framed it, is that mandamus reaches duties imposed by law, ministerial and public in character, and nothing softer.

Prohibition

Prohibition, she noted, is a writ that orders a respondent to refrain, to stop pursuing a judicial action or proceeding. Where mandamus says do, prohibition says do not; and, crucially, it does more than freeze what remains. It is "designed to prevent what remains to be done as well as to undo what has illegally been done."

In Ayad v. Dennis, a ministry that imposed a penalty without due process was restrained, and what it had already done was undone. In Broh v. House of Representatives, the writ reached the legislature itself, which had jailed an official for contempt without a hearing; she noted the principle that prohibition extends not only to ongoing acts but to threatened future ones. Togba v. Republic and the recent Yealue v. Executive Branch rounded out the picture: the writ granted to prevent some great outrage upon settled principles of law, where wrong, damage, and injury are likely to follow.

And where prohibition will not lie: it cannot be filed prematurely, before administrative and procedural remedies are exhausted (Sen. Garlawolu v. NEC); it cannot be made a substitute for an appeal that was available and abandoned (SSF Entrepreneur v. Quality Group); and it cannot be used as a stand-in for mandamus, nor will it lie where the act complained of is already complete and nothing remains to be done (Ware v. Republic).

Error

The writ of error, she explained, is how the Supreme Court calls up for the review of a judgment of a lower court from which an appeal was not announced at the time of rendition. It is, in a sense, the law's mercy for the litigant who, for good reason, missed the ordinary door of appeal, provided he has not slept on his rights.

The application is formal and she did not let the class rush past it: an assignment of error resembling a bill of exceptions, verified by affidavit that the application is not for harassment or delay; a statement of why no appeal was taken; an allegation that the judgment has not been executed; a counsellor's certificate that real errors are assigned; and payment of accrued costs, with a bond where required.

Error will issue only to a party who, for good reason, failed to appeal or lost the right without laches, the standard stated in Brown Boveri v. Lewis. But she was equally firm on its limits. A party who asks for and receives time to settle a judgment waives any defect and cannot afterward bring error (Brown Boveri again). Error cannot be made to perform the office of certiorari (Compagnie Française de l'Afrique Occidentale v. Cole). It cannot be sought by one who was never a party to the judgment (Pratt v. Badio). And one who participated in the trial is estopped from later resorting to error; where a judge proceeds by wrong rules, the remedy is prohibition, not error (Kromah v. Pearson).

Quo Warranto

She closed the substantive portion with the writ that, of the five, feels most like a question put directly to a person in power: by what authority?

Quo warranto, she explained, may be instituted against one who usurps, intrudes into, or unlawfully holds a public office or a franchise; against an official or corporate officer who has forfeited his office; against persons acting as a corporation without being duly incorporated; and against a foreign corporation exercising rights not granted to it by law. It is brought by the Attorney General, the Minister of Justice, before a Justice of the Supreme Court, and any party may demand a jury trial in writing, but only within ten days after service of pleadings, or the right is waived.

For illustration she used Tamba v. Republic, where persons functioning as justices of the peace without a valid presidential commission were ousted, and the two Williams v. Smith rulings: the Chambers stage on the jury-demand-and-waiver rule, and the Full Bench confirming the Supreme Court's exclusive original jurisdiction over quo warranto. Title to office, she reminded us, is not a thing a holder can simply assert; the law has a writ to test it.

The Exercises

What I remember most is how she ended. She ended with problems: five short scenarios, with each asking the same simple question: which remedial writ applies?

  1. A judge who denies a motion challenging his jurisdiction.
  2. A bill of exceptions refused as untimely after counsel's sudden death.
  3. A defective appeal bond approved by the trial judge.
  4. A final judgment entered against an absent lawyer, with no appeal taken.
  5. An indictment a judge refuses to dismiss before trial.

For a few minutes the room stopped being an audience and became a class again, students reasoning aloud, defending certiorari against prohibition, weighing error against appeal. It was the truest test of whether the presentation had concluded with the best of understanding among the students, and from where I sat, it had.

A Clerk's Reflection

There is a particular feeling in returning to a lesson you once received as a student and meeting it again from the other side of the bench's shadow. The doctrine had not changed; I had. What was once a list to be memorized for an examination now read as a working map of how the Supreme Court keeps the courts below it honest: when it corrects (certiorari), when it compels (mandamus), when it forbids (prohibition), when it forgives a missed appeal (error), and when it asks by what authority a person holds office (quo warranto).

I am grateful to have accompanied Justice Wolokolie, and grateful, too, for the reminder that the best of our judges and justices never fully stop being teachers. I came expecting to learn, and I left having been taught. That, I suspect, is exactly how she wanted the day to end.
