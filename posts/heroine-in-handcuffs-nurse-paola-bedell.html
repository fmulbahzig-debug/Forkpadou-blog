A Heroine in Handcuffs? Nurse Paola Bedell and the Labelling Theory

On May 23, 2026, Nurse Paola Nyemade Bedell, a healthcare provider at the John F. Kennedy Hospital in Liberia, became the subject of a national discussion. She was seen at the headquarters of the Liberia National Police in Monrovia — not by way of invitation. She had been arrested, handcuffed, and subjected to national embarrassment. In the judgment of the Liberia National Police, she had committed a woeful act and thereby became a common criminal. What was her offence? According to public sources on social media, she made a voice recording cautioning fellow health workers about a potential Ebola symptom and posted it in a social media chatroom comprising entirely of healthcare workers. The recording became widely circulated and was reportedly interpreted in ways that caused public alarm.

Was She Really a Criminal?

That was the question that guided my lecture in Criminology 202 (CRIM 202), "Crime, Justice and Society," during the class regular lecture section on May 27, 2026. My students were divided in opinion — as the larger society has been. Those contending that she is not a criminal have presented compelling arguments. Cllr. Gbawou Kowou, a distinguished Counsellor of the Liberia National Bar Association, serving as the legal representative for Paola, had this to say:

"Paola Nyemade Bedell is not a criminal. She is a hero — a brave Liberian woman who chose conscience over silence at a moment when fear and uncertainty were growing. As a first responder and healthcare professional, she understands better than most of us the devastating consequences of delayed warnings and poor preparation."

The revered University of Liberia professor Dr. Foeday Kerkula expressed his view in verse. A few lines from his poem, titled "If Telling the Truth Is a Crime, Then I Want to Be a Criminal," read:

A nurse stood at the gates of death,
Counted the bodies the system refused to count,
Raised her voice like a lantern in a plague-dark room—
And they answered her truth
With handcuffs.

What is common to both perspectives is the view that Paola should be considered a heroine, not a criminal.

The Academic Lens: Labelling Theory

Approached through an academic lens, the case of Nurse Paola stood out as a striking example when my students and I examined crime from the perspective of labelling theory. Our thesis is that Paola is not a criminal by virtue of her action; rather, she was made a criminal by virtue of how the police applied the rules to her actions. This thesis is grounded in Howard Becker's foundational definition of deviance:

"Deviance is not a quality of the act the person commits, but rather a consequence of the application by others of rules and sanctions to an 'offender.' The deviant is one to whom that label has successfully been applied." (Becker, 1963)

Nurse Paola — described by many as a heroine — has been stamped with a criminal label. In doing so, the police projected a criminal image onto a young professional nurse. That label has now become her master status, overriding all the other identities she bears: nurse, colleague, concerned professional, whistleblower. In the framework of labelling theory, it no longer matters what she is; what matters is what she has been called.

Securitization: Why the Label Was Applied

If you asked me why that happened, I would again turn to theory — specifically, securitization. The state plainly sought to control the narrative around a potential Ebola outbreak. Securitization theory, drawn from the Copenhagen School of security studies, holds that security threats are not objective, pre-existing facts; they are constructed through deliberate speech acts and social processes. A securitizing actor — typically the state — frames an issue as an existential threat and invokes measures beyond the scope of normal institutional response.

In this case, Paola blew the trumpet, and news of that trumpet spread like a dry-season fire. There was a legitimate fear that the news of a potential Ebola symptom would be interpreted in ways that would threaten commerce and normal state functions. And so what did the police do? They labelled Paola's action as criminal — not because she committed a crime, but to signal to the larger population that Paola, and the news emanating from her, could not be taken seriously. Criminalizing her was itself a securitizing move: a way to contain the narrative by discrediting its source.

I am not convinced the criminal label accomplished that goal. I would have approached it differently. The prudent starting point would have been an internal administrative or ethical investigation — a process that would have laid the obvious foundation to trace the source of the information and produce a report offering ultimate clarity to the general public, without the spectacle of a nurse in handcuffs.

As it stands, a person whom many consider a heroine now carries a criminal label. And therein lies one of the clearest real-world illustrations of labelling theory that a Criminology classroom could ask for.
