The Price of Free?: A Reflection on Class Attendance at the University of Liberia

There is a particular kind of silence that greets an instructor when a classroom should be full and is not. Eleven minutes into a recent class, I counted five students seated among thirty-four enrolled. By the time the session ended, twelve had arrived.

I teach Criminology at the University of Liberia. This semester marks my second consecutive semester back at the lectern, and in that time I have watched a pattern repeat itself with enough consistency to demand attention. In my Monday, Wednesday, and Friday sections — classes of fifteen students — it has not been unusual to see five or fewer in attendance on any given day. The empty chairs are not an anomaly. They are the norm.

A Different University

Eight years ago, I taught Social Science at the same institution. The experience bore little resemblance to what I observe today. My classes of fifty or more were routinely filled to capacity. Students arrived early — not just on time, but ahead of time — to secure a seat and wait for the instructor to begin. The energy in those rooms was different. The expectation was different. Learning felt like something people came to pursue, not something they happened into if the morning worked out.

That version of the University of Liberia feels distant now. The question I am left carrying out of every underpopulated classroom is a simple one: what has changed?

I do not have a definitive answer. I suspect the honest answer is that no one yet does. But I have a hypothesis worth examining, and I posed it directly to my students.

The Question I Put to the Room

I asked them to consider whether the University of Liberia's free tuition policy has had an adverse effect on student attitudes toward class attendance.

The University of Liberia currently operates under a free tuition policy — students pay nothing to attend. My hypothesis, which I offered with full transparency as a hypothesis only, is that patterns of attendance might look measurably different at institutions where students pay fees of up to four hundred United States dollars per semester. Where money has been spent, something psychological shifts. The cost of not showing up becomes tangible in a way it simply is not when nothing has been paid at all.

The response from my students was, to use the precise word, fascinating.

What the Students Said

Three distinct perspectives emerged, each worth taking seriously.

The first traced the problem not to the university but to the secondary schools that feed it. High schools, this student argued, have normalised a culture of indiscipline — students arrive whenever they choose, leave when they like, and face little or no accountability from school authorities. That culture does not evaporate at graduation. Students carry it with them into university, and what was tolerated before continues to be practised now. The university, on this account, is inheriting a problem it did not create.

The second perspective pointed inward, toward the institution itself — specifically at instructors who attend their own classes irregularly. If students observe that the people responsible for teaching do not treat attendance as a serious obligation, the reasoning goes, they will mirror that behaviour. Disrespect for the classroom, on this account, flows in both directions. The instructor sets the standard, and an absent instructor sets a clear one.

The third perspective spoke directly to my hypothesis. Several students agreed that the free tuition policy may indeed be a contributing factor. There is a broadly observed truth in societies like ours: what costs nothing is often treated as worth nothing. When something is freely given, its value can become invisible to those receiving it. Applied to education, the argument is pointed — a student who pays for a seat in a classroom may sit in it very differently from one who does not.

Where This Leaves Us

None of these explanations is mutually exclusive. All three may be operating simultaneously, reinforcing one another to produce what I observe in my classroom every week. A culture of indiscipline formed in secondary school, left unchallenged by inconsistent instruction, and further softened by the absence of financial stakes — that combination tells a coherent story.

What I am most certain of is that this is not a question to be settled by classroom discussion alone. It is a subject for serious empirical research — the kind that gathers data, tests hypotheses across institutions, and gives universities something they can act upon. The University of Liberia deserves that inquiry. More importantly, the students who enrol there deserve an institution that takes the question seriously enough to ask it rigorously.

Free tuition is a laudable policy. But a policy that puts students in the building without putting them in the room has not yet finished its work.

For now, I will keep counting the chairs — and hoping the count improves.
