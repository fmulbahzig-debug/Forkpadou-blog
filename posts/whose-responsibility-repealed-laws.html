Whose Responsibility It Is To Fix The Books When Laws Are Repealed, Or Amended?

Certain statements do not easily go away, at least not in Liberia. One such statement goes: "We have no shortage of laws; our challenge is only with respect to implementation." That observation is more accurate than it might first appear, and one of its clearest illustrations is the state of our law books.

Open many of them, and you will find laws still in print that have long since been repealed or amended: no footnote, no asterisk, no indication whatsoever that the text on the page no longer carries the force of law. For some time, I have watched the practical challenge this creates for the practice of law in Liberia, and it leaves me with a straightforward question: whose task is it to remove repealed laws from existing law books?

An Observation from Practice

From my current role, I have the privilege of reviewing numerous pleadings. One pattern I observe regularly is the citation of laws that have already been repealed. This is not a minor technical error. When a lawyer cites a repealed statute in support of a legal argument, they are building on a foundation that no longer exists, and the consequences for their client can be serious.

But the problem is not entirely the lawyer's fault. If a law remains in print with no indication that it has changed, a busy lawyer conducting research will find it, rely on it, and cite it. I hold the view that the burden of knowing that the law has changed falls entirely on practicing lawyers who must be up to date with changing laws. I also hold the view that when state actors print legislation on the pages of books as a notice to the public including lawyers, it is a moral, if not statutory, obligation to remove those laws from the books to satisfy the notice that those laws no longer exist. In my frank opinion, it is very unreasonable to leave the burden of verifying whether laws on the books still have the effect of law or not solely with the lawyers.

A Classic Example: Adverse Possession

Consider the statutory period for adverse possession. Previously, Section 2.12(2) of the Civil Procedure Law of Liberia set that period at 20 years or more. That provision was changed in 2018 and expressly repealed by Article 70 of the Land Rights Act. Yet Section 2.12(2) still appears in the law books, with no footnote, no annotation. Because it remains in print, it continues to be cited in pleadings, and most times without any reference to the new controlling authority on the statutory period for adverse possession.

The New Controlling Law

The statutory period to assert a claim of adverse possession is now 15 years or more, currently governed by Article 22 of the Land Rights Act. Every lawyer handling land disputes should be aware of this change. But awareness alone cannot substitute for a properly maintained body of laws. Knowing what the law says requires first knowing that the old law has been replaced. Unfortunately, our law books currently offer no help on that point when it comes to changes made in statutes over the last 10 years.

A Broader Problem

The adverse possession example is not an isolated case. Take a careful look at most recent statutes and you will find an entire section designating repealed laws. Check those supposedly repealed laws, and they are still on the books, intact, uncrossed, and available for citation. Across multiple areas of law, the same pattern repeats: new legislation expressly repeals old provisions, yet the old provisions remain in circulation as though nothing has changed.

This is a structural problem, and it cannot be solved by asking individual lawyers to independently verify whether every statute they encounter is still valid. The solution requires deliberate institutional action.

Whose Responsibility Is It?

This is a wake-up call. The responsibility to maintain an accurate and updated body of laws does not belong to individual lawyers alone. It belongs to the state actors — the Legislature, the Law Reform Commission, and the relevant ministries and agencies — who have both the authority and the obligation to ensure that the laws of Liberia reflect what is actually in force.

If the letters on the pages have ceased to carry the force of law, they should be removed, annotated, or clearly marked as superseded. To leave them standing without any indication of their status is to guarantee the very implementation gap we so often lament. The requisite state actors must act accordingly — and the time to do so is now.
