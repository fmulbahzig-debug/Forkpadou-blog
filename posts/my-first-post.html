Why I Started This Blog

Over fourteen years of professional work — spanning advocacy, governance and compliance, the rigorous practice of law, and lecturing university courses — teaches you many things. It teaches you to read carefully, argue precisely, and sit with complexity without demanding that it resolve itself before you are ready. What it does not automatically teach you is how to speak to the people outside the room — the students, the citizens, the practitioners who are shaped by the law without always having access to the thinking behind it. This blog is my attempt to close that gap.

I am a Law Clerk at the Supreme Court of Liberia, assigned to the Chambers of Justice Wolokolie, and an Adjunct Lecturer at the University of Liberia. I hold two LLMs and an LLB from the Louis Arthur Grimes School of Law, and I am a licensed lawyer in good standing with the Liberian National Bar Association. Before my current roles, I designed and implemented governance programs for USAID Liberia on a development portfolio exceeding fifty-two million United States dollars — programs that included support for landmark legislation in Liberia, among them the Land Rights Act and the Local Government Act. I say all of this not to recite a resume, but to be transparent about the vantage point from which I write.

Two Rooms, One Blog

My professional life currently takes place in two rooms, and both of them produce more than I can carry home in my head alone.

The first room is the lecture hall at the University of Liberia, where I returned to teaching Criminology two semesters ago after an eight-year absence from the lectern — having previously taught Social Science at the same institution. The classroom has changed. The students have changed. The questions they ask — and the ones they do not ask — tell me things about university education in Liberia that deserve to be written down and examined. Every session produces a reflection worth recording: about how students learn, about the gap between what they are taught and what they carry into the world, about the issues that surface in discussion when you give a room permission to think out loud.

The second room is in the Chambers of Justice Wolokolie at the Supreme Court of Liberia. When the Court hands down an opinion, it does not merely settle the case before it — it lays down a new rule of law. Liberian jurisprudence is built opinion by opinion, and my position places me in close proximity to that process. There is a responsibility that comes with that proximity: to help the public, practitioners, and students understand what the Court has decided, what principle it has established, and what it means for the practice and development of law in Liberia.

Why Now

Good governance, transparency, and access to legal knowledge have been the foundations of every role I have held — from legislative drafting to development policy to the bench. A blog is, in its own way, an act of transparency. It is a commitment to making the thinking visible, not just the conclusion.

I have no interest in writing for its own sake. What I want is a record — of what happens in my classrooms, of what the Court is building through its opinions, of the questions that Liberian law and governance are still working to answer. If that record is useful to a student, a colleague, a policymaker, or a curious reader, then it has done its work.

The law belongs to everyone it governs. Writing about it publicly is the least we can do to honour that fact.
