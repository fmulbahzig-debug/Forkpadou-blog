Theory Meets the Cell Block: An End-of-Semester Penology Seminar on Liberia's Prison System

An end-of-semester seminar at the University of Liberia paired four months of classroom theory with the hard realities of a national prison system straining under overcrowding and mass pretrial detention.

Adjunct Lecturer, Department of Sociology, Anthropology and Criminology, University of Liberia

From the Classroom to the Cell Block

Saturday, June 13, 2026 marked the official close of classes for the Second Semester of the Academic Year 2025/2026. My Penology class (CRIM 301) ended in an unconventional way. Rather than a routine final lecture, we converted the session into an End-of-Semester Seminar and welcomed two guest lecturers drawn from the bench and the corrections establishment.

CRIM 301 students at the end-of-semester seminar, University of Liberia, June 13, 2026

The seminar was conceived as a follow-on to the class's June 6, 2026 visit to the Monrovia Central Prison (MCP), Liberia's largest detention facility. Penology, at its core, is the study of punishment and the institutions that administer it, and the visit had served its central pedagogical purpose: to let students measure nearly four months of theory against the lived realities of confinement, including the administration and supervision of inmates. Our overarching observation was encouraging in one respect and sobering in another. We saw systematic improvement in the classification of inmates, a basic safeguard of order and safety inside any facility. Yet pretrial detention remained a persistent and troubling feature of the system. On the day of our visit, the prison held 1,599 persons, of whom only 388 had been convicted. More than three-quarters of those detained, in other words, were still awaiting the judgment of a court.

Those figures dictated the agenda for our post-visit lecture series. We needed a comprehensive overview of the prison system across the entire country, and I also thought it appropriate to introduce the students to the concept of sentencing and the factors that inform it. Sentencing is the final ritual that unfolds in the courtroom before a convicted person is transferred to prison, the last institution in the chain of criminal justice. To understand the prison, the students first had to understand how people arrive there.

The First Lecture: The Logic of Sentencing

Cllr. Ernest Bana, the Stipendiary Magistrate at the Brewerville Magisterial Court, honored our invitation as the first guest lecturer. Speaking from more than fifteen years on the bench of Liberia's entry-level trial court, he lectured on sentencing with the authority of a judge who has carried real cases to conclusion, entering convictions and imposing sentences where the law required.

Sentencing, he explained, is shaped by a constellation of factors: the severity of the offense, whether the crime was committed for financial benefit, whether the offender resorted to violence, and whether the offender has a prior record. To the trained ear, these considerations track the classic purposes of punishment that the course had examined all semester, from proportionate retribution to deterrence and incapacitation. Through his illustrations, the students came to appreciate a principle that often surprises the layperson: similar offenses, committed by different people, may yield markedly different sentences once the underlying circumstances are weighed. The class is deeply grateful to Cllr. Bana for making that principle concrete.

A Statute and a Rule in Tension

The transition to our second lecturer arose naturally from the discussion. Responding to a student's question on whether there is a statutory period within which a detained person must be tried, Magistrate Bana pointed to Section 18.2 of the Criminal Procedure Law of Liberia. Its operative sentence reads:

A court shall dismiss a complaint charging a defendant with an offense triable by a magistrate or justice of the peace if trial is not commenced within fifteen days after the arrest of the defendant or his appearance in court in response to a summons or notice to appear.

He set that statute alongside Rule 19 of the Rules and Regulations Governing the Magistrate and Traffic Courts, which provides:

Any prisoner duly arrested in a criminal case in the Magistrate's Court or Traffic Court, and whose case shall remain untried for ten days shall be entitled to jail delivery, and such person so entitled shall be sent up by the Jailor to the committing court to be discharged according to law, except upon request of the City Solicitor of the Court and where it is determined that the safety of the State requires the further detention of the prisoner(s).

A rich discussion followed over whether the Regulation conflicts with the Statute. On a careful reading, the two provisions address different moments in the life of a case. Section 18.2 fixes a fifteen-day deadline for the commencement of trial and prescribes dismissal where that deadline is missed. Rule 19 speaks to a case that remains untried for ten days and directs that the prisoner be released, subject to a public-safety exception invoked by the City Solicitor. As a matter of the hierarchy of laws, a subordinate regulation must yield to a statute wherever the two genuinely collide, so the fifteen-day standard would prevail in any true conflict. The more practical reading, however, is that the provisions can be reconciled, since one governs the start of trial and the other its uninterrupted progress. Magistrate Bana, mindful of the congested dockets that magistrates carry, took the view that a strict mandate to release a prisoner over a ten-day pause in trial is difficult to operate in practice.

The Second Lecture: Speed and the Demands of Justice

Atty. Gabriel Fayiah N'dupellar, the Ministry of Justice's Assistant Minister for Rehabilitation, took up the discussion as our second lecturer and offered a different emphasis. While he did not pronounce directly on whether the Statute and the Regulation conflict, he pressed the principle that courts ought to dispense justice speedily. A former Magistrate himself, with more than five years of service in rural Salayea, Lofa County, he drew on the rhythm of a rural docket. With at most five cases in a day, he argued, a court can reasonably dispose of a matter within four to five days, and delay becomes a choice rather than a necessity.

The exchange read, to my ear, as a high-level policy debate between two seasoned practitioners. Atty. N'dupellar's position is informed not only by his years on the bench but also by his present role, which carries supervisory oversight of the very prison system whose population swells with each adjournment. It was a fitting prelude to the presentation that followed.

A Nation's Prisons in One Hour

"Over one month's lesson was compressed into one hour."

A CRIM 301 student, on Atty. N'dupellar's presentation

The Assistant Minister's presentation made a lasting impression. As one student confessed afterward, "Over one month's lesson was compressed into one hour." It was less a complaint than a tribute to how efficiently he summarized the national picture and tied it to what the students had witnessed at the MCP on June 6.

CRIM 301 students at the close of the seminar, University of Liberia, June 13, 2026

He traced Liberia's prison system to 1910 and the establishment of the infamous Belleh Yealah Prison, set deep in the forest of what is today Gbarpolu County. The word "infamous" has always clung to that place. Our national history records how figures who challenged the regimes of the True Whig Party oligarchy and, later, the military junta of Master Sergeant Samuel K. Doe were confined there, far from public view. The prison's eighty-year history closed in 1990 with the onset of the Liberian Civil War.

The infrastructural strain that defined the old system endures in the present one. Our visit established it at the MCP, and the presentation confirmed the same pattern nationwide. The facility known as the National Palace in Zwedru, Grand Gedeh County, with a capacity of 270 inmates and the most substantial prison infrastructure in the country, is the only prison in Liberia currently operating within its capacity. Everywhere else, the picture is one of overcrowding and mass pretrial detention. As of June 11, 2026, the nationwide prison population stood at 3,698 persons held in facilities built for only 1,350, an occupancy of more than 270 percent. Of that population, just 1,119 were convicted and sentenced. The remaining 2,579, roughly seven in every ten detainees, were pretrial detainees awaiting their day in court.

Signs of Progress

Whether the twin burdens of overcrowding and prolonged pretrial detention will ease soon remains uncertain, but the Assistant Minister pointed to several promising developments. A modern prison is under construction at Cheesemanburg Township, Montserrado County, a project initiated in 2014 on twenty-eight acres deeded to the Ministry of Justice. A further thirty acres in Bensonville have been set aside for juvenile reformatories, a meaningful step toward separating young offenders from the adult population.

Of all the developments described, it was the digitalization of prison records that captured my attention most. During our visit we received an orientation on the record system, and the presentation explained that the Bureau of Corrections and Rehabilitation now operates a National Prison Database, developed by Impact Evaluation with funding from the U.S. Bureau of International Narcotics and Law Enforcement Affairs (INL) and the U.S. Embassy, to maintain real-time electronic records nationwide. Accurate, current data is the precondition for almost every reform that might follow, from managing capacity to monitoring how long the unconvicted remain behind bars.

The Budget Problem

Yet a single figure threatens all of this. The greatest obstacle to these projects, and to the modernization of the prison system as a whole, is the funding status of the Bureau of Corrections and Rehabilitation (BCR). Its allocation in the 2026 national budget is roughly US$800,000, a drop in the ocean. Measured against the allocations to the police, at US$25.79 million, and the judiciary, at about US$32 million, the correctional component of the criminal justice process stands as a dwarf beside its siblings. Corrections is where the system's promises are ultimately kept or broken, and it is funded as though it were an afterthought.

The Judiciary

If this pattern holds over the next three years, the conclusion is difficult to avoid: a prison system in decline, asked to do more with less while its population climbs. The seminar ended, as good seminars often do, not with tidy answers but with a sharpened question. What does it say about a justice system when its courts, its police, and its laws are all invoked to send people to an institution the State has chosen not to fund? My students left with that question in hand. So, I suspect, will the policymakers who must eventually answer it.
