About Me

I'm F. Mulbah Zig Forkpa, Jr. — an accomplished legal professional with over 14 years of progressive experience in legal advisory, legislative drafting, contract management, regulatory compliance, and institutional representation. Welcome to my corner of the internet.

I currently serve as Law Clerk at the Supreme Court of Liberia, assigned to the Chambers of Justice Wolokolie, and as an Adjunct Lecturer at the University of Liberia — two roles that together place me at the crossroads of living jurisprudence and legal education every single day. Prior to this, I provided strategic legal guidance to USAID Liberia on a development portfolio exceeding $52 million, where my work directly shaped programme integrity and compliance frameworks on some of the country's most significant development initiatives.

Education & Credentials

I hold two LLMs (Master of Laws) and an LLB from the Louis Arthur Grimes School of Law — the premier law school of Liberia. I am a Licensed Counselor-at-Law in good standing with the Liberian National Bar Association.

What I Do

My professional record spans the drafting of landmark legislation, high-stakes policy advocacy, and the representation of institutional interests in complex regulatory environments. I have built a distinguished track record working across the legal, diplomatic, and development sectors in Liberia.

Alongside my work at the Supreme Court, I teach at the University of Liberia, where I present courses covering core legal principles, governance frameworks, and the real-world application of law in the Liberian context. The classroom is where I bring together everything I have practised — translating years of courtroom and policy experience into lessons that the next generation of Liberian legal professionals can carry forward.

I am recognised for my integrity, credibility, and an unwavering commitment to good governance, transparency, and sustainable development — values that have guided my contributions to the Ministry of Finance and Development Planning and that I bring into every lecture I deliver.

Why This Blog

This blog has two distinct sources — the lecture hall and the chambers — and both feed it in equal measure.

From the University of Liberia, it draws its teaching journal: academic reviews of the lessons I present, reflections on the issues and perspectives that surface in class discussion, and honest accounts of the legal and governance questions my students and I wrestle with together. Every lecture produces something worth recording — a concept that sparked unexpected debate, a student's insight that reframed the room, a principle that suddenly connected to something happening outside the window. Those moments belong in writing, not just memory.

From the Chambers of Justice Wolokolie, it draws something rarer: a front-row perspective on Liberian jurisprudence as it develops in real time. When the Supreme Court hands down an opinion, it does not merely settle a dispute — it lays down a new rule of law. Through case law, the Court continuously shapes what the law of Liberia is and how it will be applied going forward. My position places me in close proximity to that process, and this blog is where I will offer considered, analytical perspectives on those emerging rules — what the Court decided, what legal principle it established, and what that means for practitioners, students, and the public.

If you are a student, a legal practitioner, a policy thinker, or simply someone curious about the development of law and governance in Liberia — this blog is written with you in mind.
